Erwin Olaf: I Am

Di Chiara Bardelli Nonino17 maggio 2019

An interview with Dutch artist Erwin Olaf looking back at 4 decades in photography creating "spider webs of beauty"

Born and raised in the Netherlands, Erwin Olaf’s aesthetic is a strange mix of Dutch Golden Age perfection and modern day disquiet. This year the acclaimed photographer is turning 60 and has a multiple-venue retrospective in three major museums, plus a monograph published by Aperture.

17 Immagini

Erwin Olaf: I am

In honor of Erwin Olaf’s sixtieth birthday, Aperture presents the first comprehensive survey of his work

Looking back at your career until now, what are your thoughts?
I don’t really like to look back but now that I had to, I have to say that I couldn’t have asked for anything better: this is exactly how I dreamt my career would go. And I am happy that I chose photography, it really allowed me to see who I was in the past, what my thoughts were: my work reads like a diary. So I look at it and I go: “Uh look how aggressive I was here, here I was clearly fighting with sexual issues, here I was so unhappy about myself, look at me trying to be an alpha male at the top of the rock, fighting all the monkeys down” and so on…

There’s a quote by Joan Didion that says: “I think we are well-advised to keep on nodding terms with the people we used to be, whether we find them attractive company or not. Otherwise they turn up unannounced and surprise us, come hammering on the mind's door at 4 a.m. of a bad night…” Are you on nodding terms with the person you used to be?
When I look at my early works I see a very dear nephew that I like, that has so many conquests to do. So insecure, so ambitious… It touches me but that’s not who I am anymore. I’ve definitely changed a lot.

How did you move from tackling social issues and taboos to a more introspective point of view?
I think it’s a matter of personal growth. And it’s also about the period you are living: when I started pursuing photography, in 1978, the sexual revolution that started in the late 60s was just hitting all the common people like me, a provincial boy. And it was also a very violent time - Ireland, Germany and Italy were all swept by terrorism, while in art you had the beginning of the punk movement, you had Robert Mapplethorpe, Joel-Peter Witkin...It was a very interesting time for a boy just out of puberty, who had just left home and started to discover the world. I wanted to conquer this world, I was addicted to nightlife and I was meeting all of the queens and kings of the night, photographing them. The revolution was also hitting the magazines: you had Helmut Newton portraying women wearing just a fur coat, with their pubic hair showing!

Now thanks to social media censorship, it seems that nudity is being removed from public consciousness...
Honestly, I wipe my ass with Facebook. I am a private person and I left these things like 7 years ago... Also, when you go on the internet, explicit sex is everywhere. At 15 you already know and have seen everything.

Do you think that art is moving away from tackling sexuality and nude because it’s so easily available on the internet?
There’s such a big distance between what you see on the screen and what you actually do, but art is definitely becoming more conservative. I’ve recently done this series called "Skin Deep" that goes back to the nude, showing the pubic areas, the pussy and the dick, because I don’t want nudity to be stolen by pornography. A naked body is something natural and there’s nothing wrong in enjoying sexuality, nothing wrong to show it in art. If you go to any museum, you’ll see naked statues and paintings. Our whole life, education, the history of European art shows that the body is something normal, natural, just a part of our existence.

What is the role of the artist at present?
Some parts of the world are becoming less and less open minded and I think artists should take a stand about it. As an artist, you can’t only look at your belly button and think it’s only about you! In my exhibitions, I always try to have one room that is really politically oriented. When I was younger my way of communicating was much more up-front but now that I am older, I try to work in a more subtle way: I try to give some clues and then you can make your own stories and make up your own mind.

What kind of advice would you give a young photographer?
What many young photographers think now is that people will get bored with their style very quickly but I think that as soon as you find a style and a language, you need to stick to it for at least 5 years. You need at least 5 years to become really good at something, and we need 5 years to see you.

For your 60th birthday, Aperture is releasing “the first comprehensive survey” of your work titled “Erwin Olaf: I Am”. How did you edit works spanning from such different decades?
As I said before, I think it reads like a diary. At the beginning, I was thinking that I could mix decades together, that an image from 1985 could work with one from 2006, but it turned out so messy: it’s the nephew and the old man thing again. Lesley Martin suggested that the order should be chronological and asked to edit it, because I hadn't enough distance. And the chronological order makes you read it like it is somebody’s life spread out in front of you: hence the title “I am”. I can’t do better than this, this is who I am, this is who I was.

In your recent series "Palm Springs" you moved out of the studio for the first time. How was it like to create your fantasy world in real settings?
I really enjoyed it very very much. After 15 years working in the studio with designed sets, where you have to make up your mind upfront to know every little detail beforehand, you go outside on location and… You can’t control everything. So you get many little presents and a lot of surprises - not always good ones, like rain or wind.. But when I am outside, even if I don’t really know exactly in which direction I’m going, even if I don’t know exactly what I am telling, I think, “Oh well, this is still me!”

Is photography a form of therapy to you?
More a way to bring order my thoughts, although some of my self-portraits have worked as a form of therapy. Not in the same sense as a shrink, but as a first step towards acceptance. In 1996 I was diagnosed with lung emphysema, so when later I made these 3 self-portraits, “I Wish, I Am, I Will Be”, the last one shows me intubated. Yes it’s fictional, but when I looked at it I saw my future. And even if it was sad, it was also the first moment I started acknowledging and dealing with it.

You often say that you are interested in creating your dream world - where does the unsettling, disturbing quality of your images come from?
Because otherwise they are boring! I don’t know if “unsettling” is the right word though. They are just as life is. We all claim happiness, we all want to be happy and on top of things but that’s not what life is most of the time. It’s not that I want to photograph unhappiness, but I want to photograph emotions I actually feel and life can be uncomfortable. There are moments in which you feel lonely even if you are surrounded by your loved ones. So I create this seemingly perfect world, with tools I learned from advertising and fashion, and then I create the crack in the wall, something imperfect crawling in.

Incorporating the glossy language typical of fashion and advertising is something we are seeing more and more in art, but when you did it, it was quite revolutionary.
Yes and the art world still doesn’t like it. But up until the Romantic era it was really normal to work for clients on assignments, and I learned a lot from working for companies. I learned to seduce the viewer.

Like a spider web…
Exactly! I create spider webs of beauty. And then when you get caught, you ask yourself “What is really going on here?”. I want you to come into my exhibition with a certain mood and come out with a different one - possibly enriched. I think this is the task of art. When art is hanged on the wall and people you don’t know are touched by it … I think that’s the greatest recognition someone can have in life.
