PM's power move in the Pacific

Scott Morrison's trip to Honiara is another example of Australia's Pacific "step up". But China is doing its own step up in the region and will regard Australia as trying to limit its influence.

Jennifer HewettColumnist
May 27, 2019 — 5.53pm

After the relentlessly domestic focus of the election campaign, Scott Morrison’s political lens is immediately broadening out to include Australia’s most pressing regional concerns.

Making a trip to the Solomon Islands his first stop as elected Prime Minister is designed to attract attention to Australia’s belated attempt to make the Pacific “front and centre of our strategic outlook”. According to Morrison, the Solomon Islands is a “key member of our Pacific family” – just one that has not had an Australian prime ministerial visit for over a decade.

Yet the diplomatic delicacy comes from trying to soften the impression Australia’s more recent interest is all about trying to ward off – or at least limit – China’s warm embrace of Pacific nations over the past few years. Even so, Australia, in an area where it can claim to exercise influence as a relatively big power, will be simultaneously attempting to have just that impact on China’s spreading authority.

The Morrison government still wants to achieve its aims without spelling them out too openly and further offending China – and without making the neighbours think they are just pawns in a bigger play. But such a political balancing act is proving increasingly difficult.

It won’t do much to improve Australia’s strained diplomatic relationship with Beijing.

The government’s “step up” into the Pacific, now reflected in also having a separate ministry under Alex Hawke, coincides with China’s efforts to step up its activities there a lot more too. This includes persuading Taiwan’s supporters in the Pacific to swap their official diplomatic allegiances. The economic equation is obvious enough.

So Solomon Islands Prime Minister Manessah Sogavare is under pressure from within his own government to cut ties with Taipei in order to encourage more infrastructure spending from China. There are similar choices facing other small Pacific nations due to their history of maintaining diplomatic relations with Taiwan rather than China.

Australia, of course, made its own choice about recognising China over Taiwan decades ago. The Joint Communique of 1972 signed by the Whitlam government acknowledged Australia recognised that Taiwan did not have the status of a national government. Despite economic and trade links between Australia and Taiwan, the acceptance Taiwan is not a sovereign state remains the official Australian line – as it does of most other countries.

But China is determined to stamp out any last vestiges of Taiwan’s diplomatic status, including in small nations of the Pacific. Last year, this logic also resulted in forcing foreign airlines, such as Qantas, to remove any suggestion from their websites that Taiwan, Hong Kong or Macau were independent of China as opposed to Chinese territories. China’s pressure tactics on Qantas attracted some criticism from the Australian government but Alan Joyce, like the CEOs of other non-US airlines, quickly agreed to Beijing’s ultimatum.

It seems probable Pacific nations will make similar judgments about their economic interests despite any push from the US – or Australia. After all, Australia can hardly suggest other governments should not do what it has done. There would also need to be a massive and unlikely increase in Australian aid projects or other funding initiatives to come anywhere near what’s potentially on offer from the Chinese.

Strained Beijing ties

But Morrison is still making clear his government’s approach to international relations will be firmly based in the region, raising Australia’s level of political and economic engagement within the Pacific.

To the extent this is interpreted by Beijing as any attempt at “containment” of Chinese influence in the Pacific, it won’t do much to improve Australia’s strained diplomatic relationship with Beijing.

Morrison won’t repeat his verbal slip-up during the campaign of referring to China as Australia’s major “customer” in attempting to explain its ability to deal with both its US “friend” and China. Some business leaders are also using the annual AFL match in Shanghai this weekend to boost Australian connections in China via a range of related events and celebrations.

But the Chinese not only regard their right to Taiwan as absolute, they are happy to make Australia feel uncomfortable about the impact of its own policy choices and more careful about its own use of language.

Australia’s humiliation of Huawei in banning its involvement in 5G remains a particular sore point. That’s even more pronounced since China and Huawei have had to grapple with the Trump administration’s willingness to dramatically ramp up restrictions on companies doing business with the Chinese telco giant.

That confrontation over technology is unlikely to end neatly but China is still avoiding upping the ante too much with the US. It may be possible to alter Huawei’s treatment in some larger trade deal – as Donald Trump occasionally suggests.

It’s much safer to be seen to be punishing Australia – almost by proxy. More importantly, that becomes a useful example of the economic risks that can come from offending a powerful China.

The Chinese burn on Australia remains restrained, however. The decline in Chinese investment here was already under way and that fall hasn’t led to a spillover into other areas of trade – from education services to iron ore. The impact is most obvious in the unexplained delays in getting Australian coal landed into Chinese ports and in the virulent tone of Chinese media.

Morrison is still unlikely to find himself being welcomed in Beijing unless he presides over a public shift that suits China. This could include signing up to the Belt and Road Initiative on infrastructure as a gesture of Australia’s good intentions about working co-operatively with its Chinese “partner”. But Morrison has given no indication he will do more than assess any involvement on a project-by-project basis.

A visit to Honiara en route to London is just another style indicator. Morrison’s political route consists of small stops and small steps rather than direct flights.
